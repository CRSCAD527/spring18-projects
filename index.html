Sentinel 2B Image Analysis: 2017 hurricane impacts on vegetative cover, Vieques, Puerto Rico

In September 2017, Puerto Rico was hit with two major hurricanes, damaging infrastructure, property and vegetation on the main island and its two smaller islands to the east. This analysis utilizes Sentinel 2B imagery to ascertain vegetative cover damage from these hurricanes on one of these smaller islands, Vieques. The analysis utilizes Normalized Difference Vegetation Index (NDVI), an indicator of vegetation health, for both pre- and post-hurricane imagery periods. This analysis focused on three coastal geographies on the island of Vieques: two portions of the Vieques National Wildlife Refuge (northwest corner and southeast shoreline), and the Bioluminescent Bay Natural Reserve on the south shore. All demonstrate vegetation loss that could be attributed to the hurricanes of 2017.

The Correlation Between Low-income Neighborhoods and School Closures in Washington, DC

In recent years, District of Columbia Public Schools have received praise for increasing graduation rates and stricter disciplinary actions. However, another trend in the system is numerous school closures. Utilizing spatial overlay analysis with US Census median income values and school closure locations, this project found closures tend to be concentrated in the lower half of the income range across Washington DC, raising concern that inequities of resource allocation may be present or even increasing within the DC school system.

Mapping Flood Risk and Urban Development in Boston, Massachusetts

Boston, Massachusetts has continued to experience both frequent flooding events and continued urban development pressure in its urban core. With new construction and commericial enterprises as well as flooding inundation, flood risk continues to increase in the city. This project uses overlay analysis and thematic mapping to visualize the intersect between development areas and increasing flood vulnerability throughout the City of Boston.

A spatial analysis of Mass Shootings in United States, 1966 - Present

Gun homicide rates in the United States are 25.2 times higher than other high-income countries, and between 1966 and 2018, 270 US shootings resulted in five or more people killed or injured (mass shootings events). 796 people were killed and 652 were injured in these shootings, with 54% of those killed and injured between 2013 and 2018. In addition to the temporal increase, this project looks at the spatial distribution of these mass shooting events, and considers their relationship to political party affiliation as well as state gun laws.

Climate change, storm surge vulnerability and public housing in Boston, Massachusetts

Even as Boston is one of the most affluent cities in the United States, it is also rife with economic disparity. With global climate change and coastal flooding, this disparity is exacerbated, putting already vulnerable populations at further physical risk during storm surge events. This project seeks to ascertain increasing risk and exposure vulnerability at income thresholds in Boston's urban neighborhoods and public housing infrastructure.

Volcanic hazard frequency, distribution and proximity to global urban centers

While volcanoes tend to be relatively rare events, vulnerability is compounded with close proximity to dense urban areas. This project sought to ascertain volcano activity and its proximity to human population centers through spatial analysis and thematic maps, highlighting areas where volcanic hazard is the highest - Central America, South America, Southeast Asia, Western Europe, and Oceania.
